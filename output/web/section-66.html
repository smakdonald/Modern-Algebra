    Section B.1 Group Theory

    Subsection Divisible Groups (*)

    Definition B.1. Divisible Group.

    An additive group \(G\) is divisible if for every \(g \in G\) and every nonzero integer \(n\text{,}\) there exists an element \(h \in G\) such that \(nh = g\text{.}\)

    Example B.2. Examples of Divisble Groups.

    1. \((\Q,+)\) is a divisible group.
    2. Circle Group.
      \(\Q/\Z\) is a divisible group known as the circle group

    Proposition B.3. Properties of Divisible Groups.

    1. Any subgroup or quotient a divisible group is also divisible.
    2. The direct product of divisible groups is divisible.

    Definition B.5. Normal Series.

    For a group \(G\text{,}\) a normal series for \(G\) is a chain of subgroups
    \begin{equation*} \{e\} = N_0 \leq N_1 \leq N_2 \leq \cdots \leq N_k = G \end{equation*}
    for some \(k \geq 0\) such that for all \(i = 0, \cdots, k-1\) we have \(N_i \unlhd N_{i+1}\text{.}\) We write such a thing as
    \begin{equation*} \{e\} = N_0 \unlhd N_1 \unlhd N_2 \unlhd\cdots \unlhd N_k = G. \end{equation*}
    We call \(k\) the length of the normal series and the groups \(N_i/N_{i-1}\) for \(i = 1, \dots, k\) are called the factors of the normal series.

    Warning B.6.

    Beware! The order relation \(\unlhd\) is not transitive. For example, you showed that \(V \unlhd S_4\) and, since \(V\) is abelian, the subgroup \(M = \{e,(12)(34)\}\) is normal in \(V\text{.}\) But \(M\) is not normal in \(S_4\text{,}\) since for example
    \begin{equation*} (1 3) [(1 2)(3 4)] (1 3)^{-1} = (3 2)(1 4) \notin M. \end{equation*}
    So in the above definition it is not necessarily true that each \(N_i\) is normal in \(G\text{.}\)
    Here is some more terminology.

    Definition B.7. Refinement.

    A simple refinement of a normal series
    \begin{equation*} \{e\} = N_0 \unlhd N_1 \unlhd\cdots \unlhd N_k = G \end{equation*}
    of a group \(G\) is a normal series of the form
    \begin{equation*} \{e\} = N_0 \unlhd N_1 \unlhd\cdots \unlhd N_i \unlhd M \unlhd N_{i+1} \unlhd\cdots \unlhd N_k = G \end{equation*}
    for some \(i\) and some normal subgroup \(M\) of \(N_{i+1}\) that contains \(N_i\text{.}\)
    A refinement of a normal is a normal series obtained by an iteration of simple refinements.

    Definition B.8. Equivalent Normal Series.

    Two normal series
    \begin{equation*} \{e\} = M_0 \unlhd M_1 \unlhd M_2 \unlhd\cdots \unlhd M_j = G \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{equation*} \{e\} = N_0 \unlhd N_1 \unlhd N_2 \unlhd\cdots \unlhd N_k = G \end{equation*}
    of the same group \(G\) are called equivalent if \(j = k\) and the factors are isomorphic up to order ‚Äî i.e., if for some \(\sigma\in S_j\) there exists an isomorphism
    \begin{equation*} M_i/M_{i-1} \cong N_{\sigma(i)}/N_{\sigma(i) - 1} \end{equation*}
    for all \(i = 1, \dots , j\text{.}\)

    Definition B.9.

    A normal series is called a composition series if each subquotient \(N_i/N_{i-1}\) is a simple group.

    Theorem B.10. Analogue of the FTA for Group Theory.

    Every finite group has a composition series. Moreover, any two composition series for the same (not necessairly finite group) are equivalent.

    Proof.

    • Existence of Composition Series for Finite Groups.
      Assume \(G\) is a finite group. If \(G = \{e\}\) then taking \(k = 0\) in the definition gives a composition series of \(G\text{.}\) If \(G\) is simple, then taking \(k=1\) does.
      Otherwise, thre is a non-trivial and proper normal subgroup \(N\) of \(G\text{:}\)
      \begin{equation*} e \lhd N \lhd G. \end{equation*}
      By induction on \(\# G\text{,}\) both \(N\) and \(G/N\) have composition series, and so by the Proposition, so does \(G\text{.}\)
    • Uniquesness of Composition Series up to Equivalence.
      Suppose we are given two composition series
      \begin{equation*} \{e\} = N_0 \unlhd N_1 \unlhd N_2 \unlhd\cdots \unlhd N_k = G \end{equation*}
      and
      \begin{equation*} \{e\} = M_0 \unlhd N_1 \unlhd N_2 \unlhd\cdots \unlhd M_j = G \end{equation*}
      for the same group \(G\text{.}\) By the previous Theorem, they admit equivalent refinements. But, by the 4-th Isomorphism Theorem, since \(N_i/N_{i-1}\) is simple, there are no normal subgroups of \(N_i\) that contain \(N_{i-1}\) besides \(N_i\) and \(N_{i-1}\text{.}\) So, the only refinements of a composition series are those obtained by including repetitions of the subgroups. This shows that the list of factors in these refinements are the original factors along with copies of the trivial group. Since the refinements are equivalent, the number of non-trivial factors in each is the same, and these non-trivial factors are precisely the factors occuring in the two original normal series.

    Definition B.11. Composition Factors.

    If a group \(G\) has a composition series (for example, if \(G\) is finite), then the factors of such a composition series are called the composition factors of \(G\text{.}\) They are well-defined up to ordering and isomorphism, thanks to Theorem¬†B.10.

    Example B.12.

    Suppose \(G = \langle x \rangle\) is cyclic of order \(n > 1\) and say \(n = p_1 \cdots p_k\) is a prime factorization of \(n\text{.}\) Then the factors of
    \begin{equation*} \{e\} = \langle x^0 \rangle \unlhd\langle x^{n/p_1} \rangle \unlhd\langle x^{n/p_1p_2} \rangle \unlhd\cdots\unlhd\langle x^{n/p_1p_2 \cdots p_k}\rangle = G \end{equation*}
    are of order \(p_1, p_2, \dots, p_k\text{,}\) respectively, and hence are all simple group. Thus the composition factors of \(G\) are
    \begin{equation*} \mathbb{Z}/p_1, \mathbb{Z}/p_2, \cdots, \mathbb{Z}/p_k. \end{equation*}

    Example B.13.

    \(\{e\} \unlhd\langle r \rangle\unlhd D_{2n}\) is a normal series. Since \(\langle r \rangle\) is cyclic of order \(n\text{,}\) it has a composition series as in the previous example. By inserting this into the length two normal series given here, we obtain a composition series of \(D_{2n}\) with composition factors
    \begin{equation*} \mathbb{Z}/2, \mathbb{Z}/p_1, \mathbb{Z}/p_2, \cdots, \mathbb{Z}/p_k. \end{equation*}

    Proposition B.14.

    ssume \(G\) is a group and \(N \unlhd G\text{.}\) If \(N\) and \(G/N\) both have composition series, then so does \(G\text{.}\) Moreover, in this case, the list of composition factors of \(G\) is the concatenation of the lists of composition factors of \(N\) and \(G/N\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    This is a consequence of the Isomorphism Theorems. In detail, a composition series for \(G/N\) has the form
    \begin{equation*} e \unlhd M_1/N \unlhd M_2/N \unlhd\cdots \unlhd M_k/N = G/N \end{equation*}
    where \(N \unlhd M_1 \unlhd\cdots \unlhd M_k = G\text{.}\) If \(e \unlhd N_1 \unlhd\cdots \unlhd N_j = N\) is a composition seires for \(N\text{,}\) then
    \begin{equation*} e \unlhd N_1 \unlhd\cdots \unlhd N_j \unlhd M_1 \unlhd\cdots \unlhd M_k = G \end{equation*}
    is a composition series for \(G\) since \(M_i/M_{i-1} \cong (M_i/N)/(M_{i-1}/N)\) for all \(i = 1, \dots, k\text{.}\) (For the case \(i = 1\text{,}\) interpret \(M_0\) as \(N\text{.}\))

    Example B.15.

    As you showed in the homework, \(S_4\) has a normal subgroup \(V\) of order \(4\text{.}\) \(V\) has a composition series of the form
    \begin{equation*} \{e\} \unlhd\langle (1 2)(3 4) \rangle \unlhd V, \end{equation*}
    and so the composition factors of \(V\) are \(\mathbb{Z}/2, \mathbb{Z}/2\text{.}\)
    The group \(S_4/V\) has order \(6\) and it has an element of order \(3\text{,}\) namely \((1 2 3)\text{.}\) If \(G\) is any group of oder \(6\) with an element \(x\) or order \(3\text{,}\) then the composition factors of \(G\) are \(\mathbb{Z}/2, \mathbb{Z}/3\text{.}\) (In fact, every group of order \(6\) has an element of order \(3\text{.}\)) To see this, note that \(\langle x \rangle\) is normal in \(G\) since it has index two.
    Using the Proposition, the compition factors of \(S_4\) are
    \begin{equation*} \mathbb{Z}/2, \mathbb{Z}/2, \mathbb{Z}/2, \mathbb{Z}/3. \end{equation*}
    We can make this more explicit too. The subgroup of \(G/N\) generated by \((1 \, 2 \, 3) N\) corresponds to the subgroup \(M\) of \(G\) that is generated by the elements of \(V\) along with \((1 \, 2 \, 3)\text{.}\) We have
    \begin{equation*} \{e\} \unlhd\langle (1 2)(3 4) \rangle \unlhd V \unlhd M \unlhd G \end{equation*}
    with factors \(\mathbb{Z}/2, \mathbb{Z}/2, \mathbb{Z}/3, \mathbb{Z}/2\text{.}\)

    Example B.16.

    The composition factors of \(S_5\) are \(\mathbb{Z}/2\) and \(A_5\text{.}\) This holds since \(A_5 \unlhd S_5\text{,}\) \([S_5:A_5] = 2\text{,}\) and \(A_5\) is simple, facts that we will prove later.
    The uniqueness claim is harder and uses the Zassenhaus Butterfly Lemma, which I will state without proof:

    Lemma B.17. Butterly Lemma of Zassenhaus.

    If \(G\) is a group, \(A \leq G\text{,}\) \(C \leq G\text{,}\) \(B \unlhd A\text{,}\) and \(D \unlhd C\text{,}\) then \((A \cap C)B \leq G\text{,}\) \((A \cap C)D \leq G\text{,}\) \((A \cap D)B \unlhd(A \cap C)B\text{,}\) \((B \cap C)D \unlhd(A \cap C)D\) and there is an isomorphism
    \begin{equation*} (A \cap C)B /(A \cap D)B \cong (A \cap C)D/(B \cap C)D. \end{equation*}

    Theorem B.18.

    Any two normal series of a group admit equivalent refinements.

    Proof.

    Given two normal series
    \begin{equation*} \{e\} = M_0 \unlhd M_1 \unlhd M_2 \unlhd\cdots \unlhd M_j = G \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{equation*} \{e\} = N_0 \unlhd N_1 \unlhd N_2 \unlhd\cdots \unlhd N_k = G \end{equation*}
    of a group \(G\text{,}\) the idea is to insert a copy of each into the other. In more detail, we form a refinement of the first normal series by replacing the single step \(M_i \unlhd M_{i+1}\) with
    \begin{equation*} M_i(N_0 \cap M_{i+1}) \unlhd M_i(N_1 \cap M_{i+1}) \unlhd\cdots \unlhd M_i(N_k \cap M_{i+1}) \end{equation*}
    for each \(i\text{,}\) noting that \(M_i(N_0 \cap M_{i+1}) = M_i\) and \(M_i(N_k \cap M_{i+1}) = M_{i+1}\text{.}\) Create a refinement of the second normal series in an analogous way. The Butterfly Lemma implies that these two refinements are equivalent to each other.

    Exploration B.19.

    Recall \(G'\) is the subgroup of \(G\) generated by all commutators \([x,y]\) for \(x,y \in G\text{,}\) and that \(G' \unlhd G\) and that \(G/G'\) is an abelian group. Define, recurseivly, \(G^{(0)} = G\) and \(G^{(n)} = (G^{(n-1)})'\) for \(n \geq 1\text{.}\)
    Assume \(G\) is finite. Prove that if \(G^{(n)} = \{e\}\) for some \(n\text{,}\) then all the composition factors of \(G\) are cyclic of prime order.
    For a challenge, prove the converse: For a finite group \(G\text{,}\) if all the composition factors are cyclic of prime order, then \(G^{(n)} = \{e\}\) for some \(n\text{.}\)
    Finite groups with this property are called solvable.
    We close this subsection with some big-picture type comments. A normal subgroup of a group is analogous to a factor of an integer, and a composition series of a finite group is analogous to a prime factorizations of integers. In this analogy, the role of a prime number is played by a simple group. One major difference is that, unlike for prime factorizations, the list of composition factors of a group do not uniquely determine the group since, for example, \(D_{2n}\) and \(\mathbb{Z}/(2n)\) (and, for that matter, any solvable group of order \(2n\)) have the same composition factors but are certainly not isomorphic.
    Nonetheless, a major motivating idea in finite group theory is the following: In order to know everything about finite group theory, one merely needs to know:
    • a complete list of all simple groups and
    • a method of reconstructing all possible finite groups having a given list of composition factors.
    The former task was essentially accomplished by the 1980's, when all finite simple groups were classified into a handful of families along with \(26\) ‚Äúsporadic‚Äù simple groups. The latter task is probably not really possible as stated here. But, there is a large collection of properties \(\mathcal P\) of finite groups such that if \(\mathcal P\) holds for all members of a compostion series of a given group then \(\mathcal P\) also holds for the group itself. For such a property \(\mathcal P\text{,}\) it suffices to check that \(\mathcal P\) holds true for every finite simple group. This technique has been used with success.

    Subsection Solvable Groups

    Definition B.20.

    Let \(G\) be a group and \(H\sse G\text{.}\) Then \(H\) is a characteristic subgroup of \(G\) if \(\varphi(H)=H\) for all \(\varphi\in\Aut(G)\text{,}\) which we denote \(H\char G\text{.}\)

    Proposition B.21.

    • If \(H\) is a characteristic subgroup of \(G\) then \(H\nsg G\text{.}\)
    • If \(H\) is the unique subgroup of its order it is a characteristic subgroup.

    Theorem B.22. Characteristic Subgroups and Normaility.

    • If \(K\char H\) and \(H\char G\text{,}\) then \(K\char G\text{.}\)
    • If \(K\char H\) and \(H\nsg G\text{,}\) then \(K\nsg G\text{.}\)

    Theorem B.23.

    Let \(H\leq G\text{.}\) Then \(G'\leq H\) if and only if \(H\nsg G\) and \(G/H\) is abelian.

    Definition B.24. \(G^{(n)}\).

    Let \(n\geq1\) and \(G\) be a group. Then \(G^{(n)}\) is defined as \(G^0=G, G^1=G',\) etc.

    Theorem B.25. Properties of \(G^{(n)}\).

    1. \(\displaystyle G^{(n)}\char G^{(n-1)}\)
    2. If \(G^{(n)}\char G\text{,}\) then \(G^{(n)}\nsg G\)
    3. \(G^{(n-1)}/G^{(n)}\) is abelian.

    Proposition B.26. Commutator of Simple Nonabelian Group.

    If \(G\) is a simple, nonabelian group, then \(G'=G\text{.}\)
    Recall Definition¬†B.5.

    Definition B.27. Solvable Group.

    A solvable series is a series in which all of the factor groups are abelian. Group \(G\) is solvable if it has a solvable series.

    Lemma B.28. Commutators and Homomorphisms.

    Let \(\varphi:G_1\to G_2\) be a group homomorphism and \(G\leq G_1\text{.}\) Then \(\varphi(H^\ell)=\varphi(H)^\ell\) for all \(\ell\leq0\text{.}\)

    Theorem B.29. Equivalent Solvability Characterization.

    1. A group \(G\) is solvable if and only if \(G^{(n)}=\{e\}\) for some \(n\text{.}\)
    2. Let \(G\) be a group and \(H\leq G\text{.}\) Then \(G\) is solvable if and only if \(H\) is normal in \(G\) and both \(H\) and \(G/H\) are solvable.

    Theorem B.30. \(p\)-groups are Solvable.

    Any \(p\)-group is solvable.
