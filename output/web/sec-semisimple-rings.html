    Section 28.1 Semisimple Rings

    Definition 28.1.

    A ring \(R\) is left semisimple if \(R\) is semisimple as a left module over itself. \(R\) is right semisimple if \(R\) is semisimple as a right modules over itself.

    Remark 28.2.

    Recall that submodules of \(R\) are left ideals and the simple ones are the minimal (nonzero) left ideals. So, \(R\) is left semisimple if and only if \(R\) is the internal direct sum of some collection of minimal left ideals \(I_j\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} R = \bigoplus_{j \in J} I_j. \end{equation*}
    Moreover, \(R\) is f.g. as a module over itself, and so this must be a finite direct sum. So, \(R\) is left semisimple if and only if \(R\) decomposes as an internal direct sum of the form \(R = I_1 \oplus \cdots \oplus I_m\) for some finite collection \(I_1, \dots, I_m\) of minimal left ideals.

    Example 28.3.

    For any \(n \geq 0\) and division ring \(D\text{,}\) the matrix ring \(M_n(D)\) is left semisimple. This was shown earlier. It is also right semisimple.

    Example 28.4.

    If \(R=K_1 \times \cdots \times K_t\) is a finite product of fields, then each \(K_i\) is a simple \(R\)-module, and \(R\) is the direct sum of these, so \(R\) is (left) semisimple.

    Proposition 28.5.

    For a ring \(R\text{,}\) the following conditions are equivalent:
    1. \(R\) is a left semisimple ring.
    2. Every left \(R\)-module is semisimple.
    3. Every SES of left \(R\)-modules is split.
    4. Every injection \(i: M' \hookrightarrow M\) of left \(R\)-modules splits
    5. Every surjection \(p: M \twoheadrightarrow M''\) of left \(R\)-modules splits.
    6. Every left \(R\)-module is projective.
    7. Every left \(R\)-module is injective.

    Proof.

    The equivalence of (2)-(5) follows from Proposition [cross-reference to target(s) "prop93b" missing or not unique]. The equivalence of (4) and (7) follows from the characterization of injective modules in Proposition [cross-reference to target(s) "prop:injective" missing or not unique] and the equivalence of (5) and (6) follows from the characterization of projective modules in Proposition [cross-reference to target(s) "prop1111" missing or not unique]. The implication (2) \(\Rightarrow\) (1) is obvious.
    Now for \((1)\Rightarrow (2)\text{:}\) Assume (1) and let \(M\) be any left \(R\)-module. It follows from the definition that an arbitrary coproduct of semisimple modules is again semisimple, and so the free module \(\bigoplus_\Lambda R\) is semisimple for any indexing set \(\Lambda\text{.}\) By choosing a generating set of \(M\) , we may find a surjection of the form \(p: \oplus_\Lambda R \twoheadrightarrow M\text{.}\) By Corollary [cross-reference to target(s) "cor93" missing or not unique], it follows that \(M\) is semisimple since it is a quotient of a semisimple module \(M\cong \oplus_\Lambda R/\operatorname{ker}(p)\text{.}\)

    Proposition 28.6.

    Let \(R\) be a left semisimple ring and write \(R = I_1 \oplus \cdots \oplus I_m\) as an internal direct sum with \(I_1, \dots, I_m\) minimal left ideals. Let \(J_1, \dots, J_n\) be a complete list of representatives of isomorphism classes as left \(R\)-modules taken from the list \(I_1, \dots, I_m\text{;}\) so, for each \(i\) with \(1 \leq i \leq m\text{,}\) there is a unique \(j\) with \(1 \leq j \leq l\) so that \(I_i \cong J_j\) as left \(R\)-modules.
    Then every \(R\)-module is isomorphic to \(J_1^{\oplus \Lambda_1} \oplus \cdots \oplus J_n^{\oplus \Lambda_n}\) for some index sets \(\Lambda_1,\dots,\Lambda_n\text{.}\)
    If \(M\) is finitely generated, \(M\) is isomorphic to \(J_1^{\oplus e_1} \oplus \cdots \oplus J_n^{\oplus e_n}\) for a unique list \(e_1, \dots, e_n\) of nonnegative integers.

    Proof.

    If \(M\) is finitely generated there is a surjection \(R^a \twoheadrightarrow M\text{.}\) Using Proposition [cross-reference to target(s) "prop916" missing or not unique] this surjection splits, so that \(R^n \cong M \oplus N\) for some \(N\text{,}\) and each of \(M\) and \(N\) is semisimple and finitely generated. So \(M = \oplus_{i=1}^s M_i\) and \(N = \oplus_{j=1}^s N_j\) with \(M_i, N_j\) simple. Clearly \(R^n\) is isomorphic to a finite direct sum of copies of the \(J_i\)‚Äôs, and so the result follows from the Krull-Schmidt Theorem for semisimple modules.
    In the general case, we know that \(M\) is a direct sum of simple modules; if some simple summand \(N\) of \(M\) is not isomorphic to one of the \(J_i\text{,}\) then \(N\) is a finitely generated counterexample to the f.g. case.
    In short, if \(R\) is left semisimple, and we know the simple decomposition of \(R\) itself, then we have a complete classification of all \(R\)-modules: they are just direct sums of the simple summands of \(R\text{!}\)
    Much of the interest in semisimple rings arises from the following:

    Theorem 28.7. Maschke's Theorem.

    If \(K\) is a field and \(G\) is a finite group such that \(\mathrm{char}(K)\) does not divide \(|G|\text{,}\) then the group ring \(K[G]\) is left semisimple.

    Proof.

    Let \(i: N \to M\) be any injection of left \(K[G]\)-modules. It suffices to prove that there is an \(K[G]\)-linear map \(p: M \to N\) such that \(p \circ i = \mathrm{id}_N\text{.}\) By restriction of scalars along the inclusion \(K \subseteq K[G]\text{,}\) we may regard \(i\) as a \(K\)-linear map between \(K\)-vector spaces. As such it admits a \(K\)-linear splitting \(f: M \twoheadrightarrow N\) (since \(K\) is semisimple). There is no reason that \(f\) will be \(K[G]\)-linear, but we can modify it so that it becomes so: Define \(p: M \to N\) by
    \begin{equation*} p(m) = \frac{1}{|G|} \sum_{g \in G} g^{-1} f(g m). \end{equation*}
    Note that the formula makes sense since \(|G|\) is invertible in \(K\) by assumption.
    Then \(p\) is still a \(K\)-linear map (since \(f\) is \(K\)-linear and the group action is \(K\)-linear). For any \(h \in G\) we have
    \begin{equation*} p(hm) = \frac{1}{|G|} \sum_{g \in G} g^{-1} f(g hm) = \frac{1}{|G|} \sum_{x \in G} hx^{-1} f(xm) = h p(m), \end{equation*}
    where the second equality is given by identifying \(x\) with \(gh\text{.}\) These conditions ensure that \(p\) is \(K[G]\)-linear. Finally,
    \begin{equation*} p(i(n))= \frac{1}{|G|} \sum_{g \in G} g^{-1} f(g i(n)) = \frac{1}{|G|} \sum_{g \in G} g^{-1} f(i(gn)) = \frac{1}{|G|} \sum_{g \in G} g^{-1} gn = \frac{1}{|G|} \sum_{g \in G} n = n \end{equation*}
    where the second equality uses that \(i\) is \(K[G]\)-linear and the third one uses that \(f \circ i = \mathrm{id}\text{.}\)

    Remark 28.8.

    The proof actually shows that \(K[G]\) is semisimple provided \(K\) is and \(|G|\) is invertible in \(K\text{.}\)

    Example 28.9.

    The group ring \(R=\mathbb{F}_p[C_p]\) does not satisfy the hypotheses of Maschke‚Äôs theorem, since the order of the group is zero in the field. In fact, \(\mathbb{F}_p[C_p]\) is not semisimple: let \(V=\mathbb{F}_p^2\) be the \({C_p= \langle g\rangle}\) representation \(g^n \mapsto \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ \lambda n & 1\end{bmatrix}\text{;}\) i.e., as a \(\mathbb{F}_p[C_p]\)-module, we have \(g \cdot \begin{bmatrix} a \\ b\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} a \\ a+b\end{bmatrix}\text{.}\) We claim that \({U=\left\{ \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ b \end{bmatrix}\right\}}\) is the unique nonzero proper submodule of \(V\text{.}\) Let \(W\subseteq V\) be a nonzero submodule and suppose that \(U\neq W\text{.}\) Then, there is some element \(v=\begin{bmatrix} a \\ b\end{bmatrix} \in W\) with \(a\neq 0\text{.}\) Then \(v\) and \(gv\) are linearly independent, so we must have \(W=V\text{.}\) It follows that \(V\) is not semisimple: it is not simple since \(0\subsetneqq U \subsetneqq V\text{,}\) but \(V\) is not a direct sum of simple modules.
    Let \(G\) be a finite group and \(K\) a field. The representation of \(G\) corresponding to \(K[G]\) viewed as a left module over itself can be described explicitly as following: As a \(K\)-vector space, \(K[G]\) has \(G\) as a basis: \(K[G] = \oplus_{g \in G} k \cdot g\text{.}\) \(G\) acts on this vector space by permuting the basis via left multiplication: \(h \cdot (\sum_g c_g g) = \sum_g c_g (hg)\text{.}\) This is sometimes called the (left) regular representation of \(G\).

    Corollary 28.10. Corollary of Maschke's Theorem.

    If \(G\) is a finite group and \(K\) is a field such that \(\mathrm{char}(K) \nmid |G|\text{,}\) then every \(K\)-linear representation of \(G\) is a direct sum of irreducible representations, and every finite dimensional representation is uniquely a finite direct sum of irreducible ones.
    Moreover, every irreducible representation arises as a summand of the left regular representation.

    Example 28.11.

    Let \(G=C_3\text{.}\) We can use Maschke‚Äôs Theorem and the theory of semisimple rings so far to classify every representation of \(G\) over \({\mathbb{R}}\) or over \(\mathbb{C}\) (or more generally over any field of characteristic not equal to 3). In any case, the left regular representation \(V\) of \(C_3\) is the three-dimensional representation with basis \(\{1,g,g^2\}\) such that \(g\cdot 1 = g, g\cdot g=g^2, g\cdot g^2 =1\text{,}\) i.e.,
    \begin{equation*} g \mapsto \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0\end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    in this basis. Also in any case, the subspace \(W\) spanned by \(1+g+g^2\text{,}\) which is the vector \((1,1,1)\) in these coordinates, is a 1-dimensional \(G\)-stable subspace, so a simple subrepresentation. Moreover, this is the trivial representation, since this vector is fixed by \(g\text{.}\) Then \(V/W\) obtains the structure of a representation. We can take \(1+W,g+W\) as a basis for \(V/W\text{,}\) and \(g\cdot (1+W) = g+W\text{,}\) and \(g\cdot (g+W) = g^2+W = -1-g +W\text{,}\) i.e., in our coordinates,
    \begin{equation*} g\mapsto \begin{bmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ 1 & -1 \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    A \(G\)-stable subspace must correspond to an eigenvector of \(g\) (which is equivalently an eigenvector of \(g^{-1}\)). The characteristic equation of this matrix shows that the eigenvalues are precisely the primitive cube roots of unity.
    If \(K={\mathbb{R}}\) (or more generally if there are no primitive cube roots of unity), then the \(2\)-dimensional representation \(V/W\) we just found is simple since there are no stable subspaces. If \(K=\mathbb{C}\) (or more generally if there are primitive cube roots of unity), let \(\omega\) be a primitive cube root of unity. The \(2\)-dimensional representation \(V/W\) has \(\omega\) and \(\omega^2\) as eigenvalues, and there are corresponding eigenvectors, so \(V/W\) is a direct sum of two 1-dimensional stable subspaces \(T, T'\) such that \(g\cdot t= \omega t\) for all \(t\in T\) and \(g\cdot t' = \omega^2 t'\) for all \(t'\in T'\text{.}\)
    We conclude that every real representation of \(C_3\) is isomorphic to a direct sum of copies of the trivial representation and copies of \(V/W\text{.}\) That is, for any such representation \(U\text{,}\) there is a basis of \(U\text{,}\) \(\{e_\alpha \}_{\alpha\in A}\text{,}\) \(\{e'_\beta, e''_\beta\}_{\beta\in B}\text{,}\) such that \(g\cdot e_\alpha = e_\alpha\text{,}\) \(g\cdot e'_\beta = e''_\beta\text{,}\) and \(g\cdot e''_\beta = - e'_\beta - e''_\beta\text{.}\)
    We conclude that every complex representation of \(C_3\) is isomorphic to a direct sum of copies of the trivial representation, \(T\text{,}\) and \(T'\text{.}\) That is, for any such representation \(U\text{,}\) there is a basis of \(U\text{,}\) \(\{e_\alpha \}_{\alpha\in A}\text{,}\) \(\{e'_\beta\}_{\beta\in B}\text{,}\) \(\{e''_\gamma\}_{\gamma\in C}\text{,}\) such that \(g\cdot e_\alpha = e_\alpha\text{,}\) \(g\cdot e'_\beta = \omega e'_\beta\text{,}\) and \(g\cdot e''_\gamma = e''_\gamma\text{.}\)
