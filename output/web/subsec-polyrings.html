    Section 3.2 Polynomial Rings

    ‚ÄúI had a polynomial once. My doctor removed it.‚Äù
    ‚ÄïMichael Grant, Gone

    Definition 3.19.

    For any commutative ring \(R\text{,}\) the polynomial ring in the variable \(x\text{,}\) written \(R[x]\text{,}\) is the set
    \begin{equation*} R[x]=\{ a_nx^n+\ldots+a_1x+a_0\mid n\in \Z, n\geq 0, a_i\in R\} \end{equation*}
    with addition defined by
    \begin{equation*} (a_nx^n+\ldots+a_1x+a_0)+(b_nx^n+\ldots+b_1x+b_0)=(a_n+b_n)x^n+\ldots+(a_1+b_1)x+(a_0+b_0) \end{equation*}
    and multiplication defined by
    \begin{equation*} (a_nx^n+\ldots+a_1x+a_0)(b_mx^n+\ldots+b_1x+b_0)=\sum_{k=0}^{n+m}\left(\sum_{i=0}^k a_ib_{k-i}\right)x^i. \end{equation*}
    For any commutative ring \(R\text{,}\) the polynomial ring in \(x_1, \dots, x_n\text{,}\) written \(R[x_1, \dots, x_n]\text{,}\) is defined inductively as \(R[x_1, \dots, x_n]=R[x_1, \dots, x_{n-1}][x_n]\text{,}\) but more easily thought of as the set consisting of (finite) sums of the form
    \begin{equation*} R[x_1, \dots, x_n]=\left\{\sum_{e_1, \dots, e_n \in \Z_{\geq 0}} r_{e_1, \dots, e_n} x_1^{e_1} x_2^{e_2} \cdots x_n^{e_n}\right\} \end{equation*}
    with addition and multiplication defined by rules similar to the ones seen above.

    Remark 3.20.

    If \(n = 1\text{,}\) letting \(x = x_1\text{,}\) then \(R[x]\) consists of all expressions of the form \(\sum_{i=0}^\infty r_i x^i\) with \(r_i = 0\) for all but a finite number of \(i\text{.}\)

    Remark 3.21.

    One often views \(R\) as the subring of \(R[x_1, \dots, x_n]\) consisting of the constant polynomials.

    Problem 3.22.

    Let \(A\) be a ring and let \(A[x]\) be the ring of polynomials in an indeterminate \(x\text{,}\) with coefficients in \(A\text{.}\) Let
    \begin{equation*} f=a_{0}+a_{1}x+\dots+a_{n}x^{n}\in A[x]. \end{equation*}
    Prove that
    1. \(f\) is a unit in \(A[x]\) if and only if \(a_{0}\) is a unit in \(A\) and \(\adots\) are nilpotent.
    2. \(f\) is nilpotent if and only if \(a_{i}\) nilpotent for all \(i\text{.}\)
    3. \(f\) is a zerodivisor if and only if there exists \(a\neq0\) in \(A\) such that \(af=0\text{.}\)
    4. \(f\) is said to be primitive if \((a_{0},\adots)=(1)\text{.}\) Prove that if \(f,g\in A[x]\text{,}\) then \(fg\) is primitive if and only if both \(f\) and \(g\) are primitive.

    Problem 3.23.

    Generalize the results of the previous exercise to a polynomial ring \(A[\xdots]\) in several indeterminates.

    Problem 3.24.

    In \(\Z[x]\) the ideal \((2,x)\) is maximal.
    Solution.
    Consider the quotient ring \(\Z[x]/(2,x)\text{.}\) By the Third Isomorphism Theorem, and because \(\Z[x]/(x)\cong\Z\) and under this isomorphism \((2,x)/(x)\) is mapped to \((2)\text{,}\) we have (omitting some details)
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\Z[x]}{(2,x)}\cong\frac{\Z[x]/(x)}{(2,x)/(x)}\cong\Z/2, \text{ a field}. \end{equation*}
    Since the quotient ring is field, we conclude that \((2,x)\) is maximal.

    Problem 3.25.

    In \(\Z[x]\) the ideal \((2,x)\) is maximal and prime, the ideals \((2)\) and \((x)\) are prime but not maximal.
    Here is a nice application of the First Isomorphism Theorem.

    Problem 3.26.

    Consider the ring \(\R[x]\) and let \(I = (x^2 + 1)\) be the principal ideals generated by \(x^2+1\text{.}\) Since \(\R[x]\) is commutative, we have
    \begin{equation*} (x^2 + 1) = \{ g(x)(x^2 + 1) \mid g(x)\in \R[x] \}, \end{equation*}
    so \((x^2 + 1)\) is simply the collection of polynomials having \(x^2 + 1\) as a factor. I claim that \(\R[x]/(x^2 + 1)\) is isomorphic as a ring to \(\C\text{.}\) To prove this we define a map
    \begin{equation*} \phi: \R[x] \to \C \end{equation*}
    sending \(f(x)\) to \(f(i)\text{,}\) the evaluation of \(f\) at \(i\text{.}\) It is easy to check \(\phi\) is a ring homomorphism and it is onto since elements of the form \(a + bx\) in the source map to all possible complex numbers under \(\phi\text{.}\) We claim the kernel of \(\phi\) is \((x^2 +1)\text{.}\) It is clear that \(x^2 + 1 \in \ker(\phi)\) and it follows that \((x^2 + 1) \subseteq \ker(\phi),\) since \(\ker(\phi)\) is a two-sided ideal. Suppose \(\phi(f(x)) = 0\) and write \(f(x) = (x^2 + 1)q(x) + r(x)\) with degree of \(r(x)\) at most one, using the division algorithm in the polynomial ring \(\R[x]\text{.}\) So \(r(x) = a + bx\) for real numbers \(a,b\text{.}\) If \(r(x) \ne 0\text{,}\) then \(r(i) \ne 0\text{,}\) which would contradict \(f(i) = 0\text{.}\) So we must have \(r(x) = 0\) and hence \(f(x) \in (x^2 +1)\text{.}\) Applying the First Isomorphism Theorem for Rings, we get
    \begin{equation*} \R[x]/(x^2+1) \cong \C \end{equation*}
    via the map sending \(f(x) + (x^2 + 1)\) to \(f(i)\text{.}\) Intuitively, we have adjoined a formal symbol \(x\) to the real numbers, and by modding out \(x^2 + 1\) we have forced \(x\) to be a square root of \(-1\text{.}\) That is, we have adjoined \(i\) to the real numbers, obtaining \(\C\text{.}\)

    Problem 3.27.

    Consider the ideal \(J = (2, x^2 + x + 1)\) of \(\Z[x]\text{.}\) Explicitly, by Lemma we have
    \begin{equation*} J =\{ p(x)\cdot 2 + q(x)(x^2 + x+ 1) \mid p(x),q(x)\in\Z[x]\}. \end{equation*}
    Suppose we want to understand \(\Z[x]/J\text{.}\) Then the Third Isomorphism Theorem is our friend. Set \(I=(2)\) and note that \(I \subseteq J\text{,}\) and so by the Third Isomorphism Theorem
    \begin{equation*} \Z[x]/J \cong \frac{\Z[x]/I}{J/I}. \end{equation*}
    Next we express both the numerator and the denominator in better terms. I claim
    \begin{equation*} \Z[x]/I =\Z[x]/(2) \cong (\Z/2)[x]. \end{equation*}
    To see this consider the reduction homomorphism \(\rho: \Z[x] \to (\Z/2)[x]\) sending a polynomial \(p(x)\) to its reduction modulo \(2\text{.}\) The kernel of this surjective ring map is \(I\text{,}\) establishing our claim by the First Isomorphism Theorem.
    Recall that \(J/I\) denotes the image of \(J\) under the quotient map \(\pi:\Z[x]\to \Z[x]/I\text{.}\) Therefore we have
    \begin{gather*} J/I =\pi(J)=\{\pi( p(x) \cdot 2 + q(x)(x^2 + x+ 1)) \mid p(x),q(x)\in\Z[x]\} \\ =\{\pi( p(x)) \cdot \pi(2) + \pi(q(x))\pi(x^2 + x+ 1)) \mid p(x),q(x)\in\Z[x]\} \\ =\{\pi( p(x)) \cdot 0 + \pi(q(x))\pi(x^2 + x+ 1)) \mid p(x),q(x)\in\Z[x]\} \\ =\{ f(x)\pi(x^2 + x+ 1)) \mid f(x)\in\Z[x]/I\}=\left((x^2+x+1)+I\right). \end{gather*}
    In other words, J/I is the ideal generated by the coset of \(x^2+x+1\) in \(\Z[x]/(2)\text{.}\) Moreover, under the isomorphism \(\bar\rho:\Z[x]/I \to (\Z/2)[x]\) discussed above, we have that \(f(x)+I\mapsto \ov{f}(x)\text{,}\) where \(\ov{f}(x)\) denotes the reduction of the coefficients of \(f\) modulo 2. Therefore \(\bar \rho(J/I)=([1]x^2+[1]x+[1]),\) where \([1]\) denotes the congruence class of \(1\) modulo 2. Now we put everything together: consider the ring homomorphism \(\varphi=\pi'\circ \bar \rho\) where
    \begin{equation*} \Z[x]/I\stackrel{\bar \rho}{\rightarrow}(\Z/2)[x] \stackrel{\pi'}\rightarrow \frac{(\Z/2)[x]}{([1]x^2+[1]x+[1])}. \end{equation*}
    Notice that since \(\bar\rho\) and \(\pi'\) are surjective, so is \(\varphi\text{,}\) thus \(\im(\varphi)=\frac{(\Z/2)[x]}{([1]x^2+[1]x+[1])}\) and
    \begin{equation*} \ker(\varphi)=\ker(\pi'\circ \bar \rho)=\ker(\pi')=([1]x^2+[1]x+[1]) \end{equation*}
    since \(\bar\rho\) is an isomorphism and \(\pi'\) is a quotient map. Applying the First Isomorphism Theorem to \(\varphi\) gives
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\Z[x]/I}{J/I}\cong \frac{(\Z/2)[x]}{([1]x^2+[1]x+[1])} \end{equation*}
    and combining this with the Third Isomorphism Theorem further yields
    \begin{equation*} \Z[x]/J \cong \frac{(\Z/2)[x]}{([1]x^2+[1]x+[1])}. \end{equation*}

    Problem 3.28.

    It turns out that the ring \(F=\frac{(\Z/2)[x]}{([1]x^2+[1]x+[1])}\) we discussed in the previous \(\ex\) is a field and by a problem from HW 11, any field \(F\) has only two ideals \((0)\) and \(F\) itself. This implies via the Lattice Isomorphism Theorem that there are only two ideals in \((\Z/2)[x]\) which contain \(([1]x^2+[1]x+[1])\text{,}\) namely \(([1]x^2+[1]x+[1])=\pi^{-1}(0)\) and \((\Z/2)[x]=\pi^{-1}(F)\text{.}\)

    Proposition 3.29.

    If \(R\) is a domain, then
    1. \(R[x]\) is a domain
    2. for any nonzero polynomials \(f,g\in R[x]\text{,}\) \(\deg(fg)=\deg(f)+\deg(g)\)
    3. the units of \(R[x]\) are the units of \(R\) (\(R[x]^\times=R^\times\))

    Proposition 3.30.

    Let \(R\) and \(S\) be commutative rings, \(\phi: R \to S\) is a ring homomorphism and \(s_1, \dots, s_n\) arbitrary elements of \(S\text{.}\) Then there exists a unique ring homomorphism
    \begin{equation*} \tilde{\phi}: R[x_1, \dots, x_n] \to S \end{equation*}
    such that \(\tilde{\phi}|_R = \phi\) and \(\tilde{\phi}(x_i) = s_i\) for all \(i\text{,}\) namely
    \begin{equation*} \tilde{\phi}\left(\sum_{e_1, \dots, e_n \geq 0} r_{e_1, \dots, e_n} x_1^{e_1} \cdots x_n^{e_n}\right) =\sum_{e_1, \dots, e_n \geq 0} \phi(r_{e_1, \dots, e_n}) s_1^{e_1} \cdots s_n^{e_n} \end{equation*}

    Proof.

    Let‚Äôs observe first that if such a map exists it is unique. For if \(\tilde\phi: R[x_1, \dots, x_n] \to S\) is a ring map extending \(\phi\) and sending \(x_i\) to \(s_i\text{.}\) Then
    \begin{equation*} \begin{align*} \tilde\phi(\sum_{e_1, \dots, e_n \geq 0} r_{e_1, \dots, e_n} x_1^{e_1} \cdots x_n^{e_n}) &= \sum_{e_1, \dots, e_n \geq 0} \phi(r_{e_1, \dots, e_n}) \phi(x_1)^{e_1} \cdots \phi(x_n)^{e_n}) \\ &= \sum_{e_1, \dots, e_n \geq 0} \phi(r_{e_1, \dots, e_n}) s_1^{e_1} \cdots s_n^{e_n}, \end{align*} \end{equation*}
    using that \(\tilde\phi\) preserves \(+\) and \(\cdot\text{.}\)
    For existence, let's assume \(n = 1\) at first. Given \(\phi: R \to S\) and \(s \in S\text{,}\) define
    \begin{equation*} \tilde{\phi}: R[x] \to S \end{equation*}
    by
    \begin{equation*} \tilde{\phi}(\sum_i r_i x^i) = \sum_i \phi(r_i) s^i. \end{equation*}
    It is elementary (but tedious) to check \(\tilde{\phi}\) really is a ring homomorphism. The fact that it restricts to \(\phi\) is clear, however.
    For the general case, we proceed by induction on the number of variables \(n\text{.}\) The induction hypothesis shows that there is a ring homomorphism
    \begin{equation*} \psi: R[x_1, \dots, x_{n-1}] \to S \end{equation*}
    such that \(\psi|_R = \phi\) and \(\psi(x_i) = s_i\text{,}\) \(i = 1, \dots, n-1\text{.}\) Applying the \(n=1\) case to \(\psi\) gives
    \begin{equation*} \tilde{\psi}: (R[x_1, \dots, x_{n-1}])[x_n] \to S \end{equation*}
    with \(\tilde\psi|_{R[x_1, \dots, x_{n-1}]} = \psi\) and \(\tilde{\psi}(x_n) = s_n\text{.}\) Setting \(\tilde\phi=\tilde\psi\) gives a map \(\tilde{\phi}\) with the needed properties.

    Problem 3.32.

    Given a ring map \(\phi: R \to S\) between commutative rings, we may apply the Proposition to the composition \(R \xra{\phi} S \hookrightarrow S[x]\) using the element \(s = x\) of \(S[x]\) to get an induced ring map
    \begin{equation*} \tilde{\phi}: R[x] \to S[x] \end{equation*}
    that sends \(\sum_i r_i x^i\) to \(\sum_i \phi(r_i) x^i\text{.}\) That is, the map \(\tilde{\phi}\) applies \(\phi\) to the coefficients of a polynomial. This can be generalized to more than one variable in the obvious way.

    Problem 3.34.

    Find all the ideals of \(\Z[x]\) that contain \((6, x^2 + x + 1)\text{,}\) the ideal of \(\Z[x]\) generated by \(6\) and \(x^2 + x + 1\text{.}\) For each such ideal, give an explicit list of generators, and determine whether the ideal is prime, maximal, or neither.
    Solution.
    First, notice that \(x^2+x+1\) is irreducible in \(\Z[x]\) as it is a cyclotomic polynomial. Thus the possibilities are the following:
    1. \(I=(3,x^2+x+1)\text{,}\)
    2. \(J=(2,x^2+x+1)\text{,}\)
    3. \(L=(6,x^2+x+1)\text{,}\)
    4. \(\Z[x]\text{.}\)
    Notice that \(\Z[x]=(1)\text{,}\) which is neither prime nor maximal, as those ideals must be proper. The ideal \(L\) is not maximal as it is contained in both \(I\) and \(J\text{,}\) nor is it prime, given that \(3\cdot2\in L\) but neither \(3\) nor \(2\) are in \(L\text{.}\)
    Both \(I\) and \(J\) are generated with irreducible elements, making them irreducible ideals and thus maximal.

    Problem 3.37.

    Prove the ideal \(I = (2, 1 + \sqrt{-13})\) of the commutative ring \(R = \Z[\sqrt{-13}]\) is not a principal ideal.
    Solution.
    Let \(R\) be a commutative ring with unity and \(f\in R[x]\) an irreducible polynomial. We want to show that \((f)\) is a prime ideal in \(R[x]\text{.}\)
    First, note that \((f)\) is an ideal of \(R[x]\text{,}\) since it is generated by a single polynomial.
    Suppose that \(g,h\in R[x]\) with \(gh\in (f)\text{.}\) Then \(gh=fk\) for some \(k\in R[x]\text{.}\) Since \(f\) is irreducible, it cannot be written as a product of two non-constant polynomials, so either \(f\) divides \(g\) or \(f\) divides \(h\text{.}\) Without loss of generality, assume that \(f\) divides \(g\text{.}\) Then \(g=fq\) for some \(q\in R[x]\text{.}\) Substituting into \(gh=fk\) yields \(fh=fqk\text{,}\) and cancelling the factor of \(f\) gives \(h=qk\text{.}\) Thus, \(h\in (f)\text{,}\) and so \((f)\) is a prime ideal of \(R[x]\text{.}\)
