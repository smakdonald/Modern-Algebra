    Section 2.2 Generated Subgroups

    Subsection Generational Wealth

    ‚ÄúHow often misused words generate misleading thoughts.‚Äù
    ‚ÄïHerbert Spencer

    Definition 2.18. Generated Subgroup.

    Given a group \(G\) and a subset \(S\) of \(G\text{,}\) the subgroup of \(G\) generated by \(S\) is
    \begin{equation*} \langle S \rangle := \bigcap_{H \leq G, H \supseteq X} H. \end{equation*}
    If \(S=\{s\}\) is a set with one element then we write \(\langle S \rangle=\langle s \rangle\) and we refer to this as the cyclic subgroup generated by \(s\text{.}\)
    The elements of \(S\) are known as generators,

    Lemma 2.19. Elements of \(\igen S\).

    For a subset \(S\) of \(G\text{,}\) the elements of \(\langle S \rangle\) can be described as:
    \begin{equation*} \langle S \rangle = \left\{x_1^{j_1} \cdots x_m^{j_m} \mid m \geq 0, j_1, \dots, j_m \in \mathbb{Z}\text{ and }x_1, \dots, x_m \in S \right\}. \end{equation*}

    Proof.

    Let \(S= \left\{x_1^{j_1} \cdots x_m^{j_m} \mid m \geq 0, j_1, \dots, j_m \in \mathbb{Z}\text{ and }x_1, \dots, x_m \in S \right\}\text{.}\) Since \(\langle S \rangle\) is a subgroup that contains \(S\text{,}\) it is closed under products and inverses, and thus must contain all elements of \(S\text{.}\)
    For the opposite containment, we just need to show the set \(S\) really is a subgroup. We use the one step test:
    • \(S \ne \emptyset\) since we allow \(m = 0\) and declare the empty product to be \(e_G\text{.}\)
    • If \(x_1^{j_1} \cdots x_m^{j_m}\) and \(y_1^{i_1} \cdots y_n^{i_m}\) are in \(S\) then
      \begin{equation*} x_1^{j_1} \cdots x_m^{j_m}(y_1^{i_1} \cdots y_n^{i_m})^{-1}=x_1^{j_1} \cdots x_m^{j_m} y_n^{-i_m} \cdots y_1^{-i_1} \end{equation*}
      is also in \(S\text{.}\)
    Therefore \(S\leq G\) and \(X\subseteq S\) (by taking \(m=1\) and \(j_1=1\)) and by the minimality of \(\langle X \rangle\) we obtain \(\langle X \rangle\subseteq S\text{.}\)
    Lets look at some examples for context.

    Example 2.20. \(n\Z=\igen n\).

    \(n\Z=\igen n\) for every \(n\in\Z\text{.}\)

    Example 2.21. Generating \(D_{2n}\).

    \(\igen{r,s}=D_{2n}\)

    Exercise 2.23. Generating \(D_{2p}\).

    Let \(p\ge 3\) be prime and \(r,r'\) be any two distinct reflections in \(D_{2p}\text{.}\) Then \(\igen{r,r'}=D_{2p}\text{.}\)

    Exercise 2.24. Generating \(\GL_n(\R)\).

    For any integer \(n \geq 1\text{,}\) the set of column vectors \(e_i\) consisting of \(1\) in the \(i\th\) row and \(0\) elsewhere generate \(\GL_n(\R)\text{.}\) As you may recall from a linear algebra course, these are called the elementary matrices. (Bonus: what subgroup do the ‚Äútype I‚Äù elementary matrices generate?)

    Proposition 2.25. Generating \(A_n\).

    \(A_n\) is the subgroup of \(S_n\) generated by all produts of \(2\)-cyclies (disjoint or not). In symbols,
    \begin{equation*} A_n=\langle (i \ j )(k \ l) \mid i,j,k,l \in \{1, \ldots, n\}\rangle. \end{equation*}

    Remark 2.26.

    Note that in Theorem¬†1.40 we showed \(S_n\) is generated by transpositions. However, there's more than one way to generate an egg.
    We look now at generating \(S_n\text{;}\) the following lemma will be helpful.

    Lemma 2.27.

    For \(\sigma\in S_n\) and distinct intgers \(i_1, \dots, i_p\) we have
    \begin{equation*} \sigma(i_1 \, i_2 \, \cdots i_p) \sigma^{-1} = (\sigma(i_1) \, \cdots \, \sigma(i_p)). \end{equation*}
    (Note that the right-hand cycle is a cycle since \(\sigma\) is one-to-one.)

    Proof.

    To prove this, evaluate both sides at \(\sigma(i_t)\) for any \(t\) and observe that one gets \(\sigma(i_{t+1})\) (with the supscript taken modulo \(p\)) both times. This proves they agree on the set \(\sigma(\{i_1, \dots, i_p\})\text{.}\) If \(j\) is not in this set, then \((i_1 \, i_2 \, \cdots i_p)\) fixes \(\sigma^{-1}(j)\) so the left-hand side fixes \(j\text{.}\) So does the right, since \(\sigma^{-1}(j) \notin \{\sigma(i_1), \dots, \sigma(i_p)\}\text{.}\) Thus the two functions coincide on elements.

    Theorem 2.28. Generating \(S_n\).

    For \(n\ge 2\) prove that \(S_n\) is generated by \((12)\) and the \(n\)-cycle \((12\cdots n)\text{.}\)

    Remark 2.29.

    This theorem will prove surprisingly useful all the way down in Galois Theory, saving us a lot of time with our proof of unsolvable quintic polyomials.
    Lets look at another example.

    Example 2.30. Commutator Subgroup.

    Let \(G\) be a group, \(S=\{aba\inv b\inv|a,b\in G\}\text{,}\) and \(G'=\igen S\text{.}\) This is known as the commutator subgroup of \(G\text{.}\)

    Remark 2.31.

    Commutator subgroups prove invaluable when it comes to something called abelianization, which is, loosely speaking, a way of "modding out" the non-commutative part of \(G\) to obtain a new group that is abelian. This is seen rigorously in Example¬†3.26.
    A nice property of a Generated Subgroup is that once you've located the generators, you've found the whole group:

    Proposition 2.32. Generators of Subgroups.

    If \(H\) is a subgroup of \(G\) that contains \(S\text{,}\) then \(\igen S\leq H\text{.}\)
    In this way, if we can understand something about the generators of a group, we can (for the most part) extend that knowledge to the group as a whole. This is something you may have seen in the form of a basis, either for a vector space or a topological space.
    Here is a proposition that might appear to come out of the blue. ‚Äâ1‚Äâ

    Proposition 2.33. \(D_{2n}\) Generators and Unique Homomorphism.

    If \(G\) is a group and \(x,y \in G\) are elements such that \(x^n = e\text{,}\) \(y^2 = e\text{,}\) and \(yxy = x^{-1}\) for some integer \(n \geq 3\text{,}\) then there exists a unique group homomorphism \(f: D_{2n} \to G\) such that \(f(r) = x\) and \(f(s) = y\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    Recall that the elements of \(D_{2n}\) are \(e, r, \dots, r^{n-1}, s, rs, \dots, r^{n-1}s\) and that this list has no repetitions. Given \(G\text{,}\) \(x\text{,}\) and \(y\text{,}\) if \(f\) exists it is clearly unique, since we have \(f(r^i) = f(r)^i = x^i\) and \(f(r^is) = f(r)^if(s) = x^iy\text{,}\) so that the value of \(f\) on every element of \(G\) is determined by its values on just \(x\) and \(y\text{.}\)
    To prove existence, define a function \(f: D_{2n} \to G\) by \(f(r^i) = x^i\) for \(0 \leq i \leq n-1\) and \(f(r^is) = x^is\) for \(0 \leq i \leq n-1\text{.}\) Then one may painstakenly checks that \(f(ab) = f(a)f(b)\) in each of the cases \(a = r^j, b= r^i\) or \(a = r^j, b = r^is\) or \(a = r^js, b = r^i\) or \(a = r^js, b = r^is\text{.}\) For example, using that \(sr^i = r^{-i}s\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{equation*} f((r^js)(r^i)) = f(r^j(sr^i) = f(r^{j-i}s) = x^{j-i}y \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{equation*} f(r^js)f(r^i)) = x^jy x^i =x^j x^{-i} y = x^{j-i} y. \end{equation*}
    For the last string of equalities, we have used that, since \(yxy = x^{-1}\text{,}\) we have \(yx^i = x^{-i} y\) in \(G\) too. Note also that \(j-i\) might be negative, in which case \(r^{j-i}\) is really \(r^{n+j-i}\) and so really we would have \(f(r^{j-i}s) = x^{n+j-i}y\text{.}\) But this is OK since \(x^{j-i} = x^{n+j-i}\) too, since \(x^n = e\text{.}\)
    I'll skip the verifications of the other three cases.

    Remark 2.34.

    This is techincally an specific instance of the Universal Mapping Property for a Presentation, which is not covered here.
    Let's take a peak at what this might look like in practice.

    Example 2.35. \(D_{2n}\) Mapping Example.

    Consider the group \(\operatorname{GL}_2({\mathbb{R}})\) and elements
    \begin{equation*} x = \begin{bmatrix} \cos(2 \pi/n) & - \sin(2 \pi/n) \\ \sin(2 \pi/n) & \cos(2 \pi/n) \\ \end{bmatrix} { \text{ and } } y = \begin{bmatrix} -1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    Then one may check that \(x^n = e\text{,}\) \(y^2 = e\) and \(yxyx = e\text{.}\) Proposition¬†2.33 gives that there is a group homomorphism \(f: D_{2n} \to \operatorname{GL}_2({\mathbb{R}})\) such that \(f(r) = x\) and \(f(s) = y\text{.}\) This is not at all surprising, since the matrix \(x\) represents rotation by \(2 \pi/n\) radians counter-clockwise and \(y\) represents reflection about the \(y\)-axis. Indeed, one could define \(D_{2n}\) as the subgroup of \(\operatorname{GL}_2({\mathbb{R}})\) consisting of elements \(1, x, \dots, x^{n-1}, y, xy \cdots, x^{n-1}y\text{.}\)

    Exercise 2.36. Another \(D_{2n}\) Mapping.

    Let \(r,s \in D_{12}\) and \(r',s' \in D_6\text{,}\) in order to distinguish them. Find a homomorphism from \(D_{12}\) to \(D_6\) with \(f(r) = r', f(s) = s'\) and prove that it is unique.
    Solution.
    Proposition¬†2.33 gives a group homomorhism \(f: D_{12} \to D_6\text{.}\) Let \(r,s \in D_{12}\) and \(r',s' \in D_6\text{,}\) in order to distinguish them. Then since \((r')^6 = ((r')^3)^2 = e^2= e\text{,}\) there is a unique homomorphism \(f\) from \(D_{12}\) to \(D_6\) with \(f(r) = r', f(s) = s'\text{.}\) For example, \(f(r^3s) = e\text{.}\)

    Summary
