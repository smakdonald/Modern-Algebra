    Section 1.2 Order and Power

    Subsection Power

    ‚ÄúHaving power is not nearly as important as what you choose to do with it.‚Äù
    ‚ÄïRoald Dahl
    Though it might seem natural to write the product \(x\cdot x\) using the notation \(x^2\text{,}\) we have no notion of what that means in the world of groups. Do exponents behave the way we want them to in groups, or does something disasterous occur? Do exponents even exist? Do I? In this section, we attempt to answer as many of these questions as is philosophically feasible.

    Definition 1.19. \(n\th\) power.

    Let \(x\) be an element of a multiplicative group \(G\text{.}\) For \(n\in\Z\text{,}\) the \(n\th\) power \(x^n\) of \(x\) is defined recursively as follows:
    • \(x^0=e\text{,}\) \(x^1=x\text{,}\) and \(x\inv=x\inv\text{;}\) ‚Äâ1‚Äâ
    • \(x^{n+1}=x^nx\) when \(n<0\text{;}\) and
    • \(x^n=(x^{-n})\inv\) when \(n>0\text{.}\)
    If \(G\) was an additive group, then we would write \(nx\) intead of \(x^n\text{.}\) This would be called a multiple of \(x\) instead of a power.

    Theorem 1.20. Laws of Exponents.

    Let \(m,n\in\Z\) and \(x\) be an element of a group \(G\text{.}\) Then
    1. \(x^mx^n=x^{m+n}=x^nx^m\) and
    2. \((x^m)^n=x^{mn}=(x^n)^m\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    1. Let \(m > 0\) and \(n > 0\text{;}\) then by induction on \(n\) and the definition \(x^mx^n =x^{m + n}\text{.}\) Multiplying on both sides by \(x^{-m}\) we deduce that \(x^n =x^{-m}x^{m+n}\) and \(x^m = x^{m + n} x^{-n}\text{.}\) Finally inversion of the equation \(x^mx^n =x^{m + n}\) yields \(x^{-n}x^{-m} = x^{-m + (-n)}\text{.}\) Hence the law is established in all cases.
    2. If \(n > 0\text{,}\) it follows from (1) that \((x^m)^n =x^{mn}\text{.}\) Now assume that \(n < 0\text{;}\) then \((x^m)^n =((x^m)^{-n})\inv =(x^{-mn})\inv =x^{mn}\) since \(x^{-mn}x^{mn}=e\text{.}\)

    Exercise 1.21. Abelian Exponents.

    Let \(x,y\in G\text{.}\) The equation \((xy)^n=x^ny^n\) holds for all \(n\in\Z\) if and only if \(G\) is abelian.

    Subsection Order

    ‚ÄúGood order is the foundation of all things.‚Äù
    ‚ÄïEdmund Burke

    Definition 1.22. Order.

    1. In a group \(G\text{,}\) the order of an element \(x\) is the least positive integer \(n\) such that \(x^n = e\text{.}\) If no such \(n\) exists, we say \(x\) has infinite order. We write \(|x|\) for the order of \(x\text{.}\)
    2. The order of a group \(G\) is the Cardinality of the set \(G\text{,}\) denoted \(|G|\text{.}\)
    Some authors use the notation \(\#G\) or \(o(G)\) to refer to the order of a group. We won't name names, but they know who they are.

    Example 1.23. Order.

    1. \(|e|=1\) in every group \(G\text{.}\)
    2. \(|1|=n\) in \(\Z/n\)
    3. In the additive groups \(\Z\text{,}\) \(\Q\text{,}\) \(\R\text{,}\) and \(\C\) every nonzero (i.e., nonidentity) element has infinite order. ‚Äâ2‚Äâ
    4. In the additve group \(\Z/6\text{,}\) the element \(2\) has order \(3\text{,}\) as \(2+2=4\) and \(2+2+2=0\text{.}\)
    We arrive at one of the central conjectures of group theory, posed by none other than UNL's Tom "Destroyer of Worlds" Marley. Best of luck. ‚Äâ3‚Äâ

    Exercise 1.24. Marley's Conjecture.

    Every group of order \(4\) is abelian.
    Solution.
    Let \(G\) be a group with \(4\) elements, and suppose there are elements \(a,b\in G\) such that \(ab\neq ba\) for some elements \(a, b\in G\text{.}\) Since \(ab \neq ba\text{,}\) we must have \(a \neq b\text{,}\) \(a \neq e\text{,}\) and \(b\neq e\text{.}\) Since \(G\) has only \(4\) elements and \(ab \neq ba\text{,}\) either \(ab \in \{e, a, b\}\) or \(ba \in\{e, a, b\}\text{.}\) Without loss, say the former occurs. But \(ab = e\) implies \(b=a\inv\) and we know \(a\inv\) commutes with \(a\text{,}\) and hence this is not possible. If \(ab = a\text{,}\) then \(b = e\) and if \(ab = b\) then \(a = 1\text{,}\) both of which are impossible. Since \(a, b\) were arbitrary, \(G\) must be abelian.

    Theorem 1.25. Properties of Order.

    1. An element \(g\) of a group \(G\) has order \(1\) if and only if \(g=e\text{.}\)
    2. \(\displaystyle |g|=|g\inv|\)
    3. If \(g^p=e\) for \(g\neq e\) and Prime Integer \(p\text{,}\) then \(|g|=p\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    1. Let \(G\) be a group and consider an element \(a\) in \(G\) with order \(1\text{.}\) By definition, the order of an element is the smallest positive integer \(n\) such that \(a^n = e\text{,}\) where \(e\) is the identity element of \(G\text{.}\) Since the order of \(a\) is \(1\text{,}\) we have \(a^1 = a = e\text{.}\) This implies that \(a\) is equal to the identity element \(e\text{.}\)

    Exercise 1.26. \(b\) there or \(b^2=e\).

    If \(G\) is a group such every non-identity element has order \(2\text{,}\) then \(G\) is abelian.

    1. If \(|x| = n\text{,}\) then \(e, x, \dots, x^{n-1}\) are all distinct elements of \(G\text{.}\)
    2. If \(|x| = \infty\text{,}\) then \(x^i \ne x^j\) for all integers (positive or negative) \(i,j\) with \(i \ne j\text{.}\)
    3. \(|x| \leq |G|\text{.}\)
    4. If \(x^m = e\) then \(|x| \mid m\text{.}\)

    Exercise 1.28. Order Hors d'oeuvre.

    Let \(G\) be a group.
    1. Let \(g \in G\) be an element of finite order. Show that \(g^m\) has finite order for any integer \(m \geq 0\text{,}\) and in fact
      \begin{equation*} |g^m| = \frac{\lcm(m,|g|)}{m} = \frac{|g|}{\gcd(m, |g|)}. \end{equation*}
    2. Prove that for all \(g, h\) in \(G\text{,}\) \(|gh| = |hg|\) holds.

    Summary

    • The Laws of Exponents hold in groups.
    • The Order of an element \(x\) is the smallest positive integer \(n\) such that \(x^n=e\text{.}\) The order of a group is its cardinality.
    Convenient notation, isn't it? Almost like we planned it.
    Groups with this property are known as torsion-free, a concept explored in more advanced contexts.
    For the more astute reader, you may observe that this is one of the more solvable conjectures in mathematics. This is the first problem Tom assigns every time he teaches 817 and it's become a bit of a running joke.
