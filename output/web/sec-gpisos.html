    Section 2.2 Group Isomorphisms

    Subsection Isomorphisms

    ‚ÄúThe test of a first-rate intelligence is the ability to hold two opposed ideas in mind at the same time and still retain the ability to function.‚Äù
    ‚ÄïF. Scott Fitzgerald

    Definition 2.15. Group Ismorphism.

    A homomorphism \(f: G \to H\) is called an isomorphism if there exists a homomorphism \(g: H \to G\) such that \(f \circ g = \text{id}_H\) and \(g \circ f = \text{id}_G\text{.}\)
    If \(f:G\to H\) is an isomorphism, \(G\) and \(H\) are called isomorphic, written \(G\cong H\text{.}\)

    Remark 2.16.

    The meaning of the word ‚Äúisomorphism‚Äù in any context (sets, groups, rings, fields, topological spaces, whatever) is always ‚Äúa morphism that has a two-sided inverse‚Äù. In many contexts this turns out to be equivalent to the notion of being ‚Äúone-to-one and onto‚Äù. But there are contexts in which this is not the case. For example a one-to-one and onto continuous map of topological spaces need not be a homeomorphism. (A homeomorphism is a term one uses for isomorphism of topological spaces, for historical reasons.)
    Intuitively, a group isomorphism establishes a one-to-one correspondence between the elements of two groups, such that they have exactly the same algebraic structure. This means that the groups are essentially the same, up to a relabeling of their elements.
    Group isomorphisms allow us to identify different groups that have the same algebraic structure. This can simplify the study of groups by reducing the number of cases that need to be considered, and it can also provide insight into the connections between seemingly unrelated groups.

    Theorem 2.17. Equivalent Isomorphism Characterization.

    Suppose \(\varphi: G \to H\) is a group homomorphism. Then \(\varphi\) an isomorphism if and only if \(\varphi\) is bijective (one-to-one and onto).

    Proof.

    We start by recalling that a function \(\alpha: X \to Y\) between two sets is bijective if and only if there is a function \(\beta: Y \to X\) such that \(\alpha\circ \beta= \operatorname{id}_Y\) and \(\beta\circ \alpha= \operatorname{id}_X\text{.}\)
    The \(\Leftarrow\) is thus immediate. For the \(\Rightarrow\) direction, if \(f\) is bijective homomorphism, then it certainly has a set-theoretic two-sided inverse \(g\text{.}\) But we need to show \(g\) is actually a homomorphism: for \(x,y \in H\) we have \(f(g(xy)) = xy = f(g(x))f(g(y)) = f(g(x)g(y))\text{.}\) Since \(f\) is one-to-one, \(g(xy) = g(x)g(y)\text{.}\)

    Remark 2.18.

    Moving forward, we will essentially treat Theorem¬†2.17 as the de-facto definition of a group isomorphism. Rather than end every proof with a reference to Theorem¬†2.17, we will instead enshrine its monumental achievements of Theorem¬†2.17 within this remark. To alleviate some of the corresponding guilt asociated with this decision I am referencing Theorem¬†2.17 as many times as possible, as Theorem¬†2.17 is important and Theorem¬†2.17 should not be forgotten.

    Example 2.19. Isomorphism Examples.

    1. The identity map is a group isomomorphism for any group \(G\text{.}\)
    2. \((\Z,+)\cong(\E,+)\text{,}\) where \(\E\) denotes the set of even integers.
    3. \(\displaystyle (\R,+)\cong(\R^\times,\cdot)\)
    4. Let \(C_\infty\) be a cyclic group with infinite elements. Then \(C_\infty\cong\Z\text{.}\)
    5. The exponential and natural logarithm maps from Example¬†2.2 are inverses, making them isomorphisms as well.

    Exploration 2.20. Complex Conjugation Automorphism.

    Prove that the function \(\phi:\C\to\C\) given by \(\phi(a+bi)=a-bi\) is an automorphism.

    Exploration 2.21. Opposite Opposite Day.

    Let \(G\) be a group and \(G^{op}\) as defined in Exploration¬†1.16 Then \(G\cong G^{op}\text{.}\)

    Exploration 2.22. Something's Missing.

    Justify why the following pairs of groups are not isomorphic.
    1. \(Q_8\) and \(D_8\)
    2. \(\Z/6\) and \(S_3\)
    3. \(D_{24}\) and \(S_4\)
    4. \(\Q\) and \(\Q^\times\)
    Now seems like an exceptional time to recall Theorem¬†2.6.

    Corollary 2.23. Composition of Isomorphisms.

    The composition of two isomorphisms is an isomorphism.

    Exploration 2.24. Isomorphisms form Equivalence Relation.

    1. \(\displaystyle G\cong G\)
    2. If \(G\cong H\text{,}\) then \(H\cong G\)
    3. If \(G\cong H\) and \(H\cong K\text{,}\) then \(G\cong K\)

    Subsection Automorphisms

    ‚ÄúSelf preservation is the first law of nature.‚Äù
    ‚ÄïSamuel Butler

    Definition 2.25. Group Automorphism.

    Let \(G\) be a group. An isomorphism \(\varphi:G\to G\) is called an automorphism of \(G\text{.}\) The set of automorphisms of a group \(G\) is called the automorphism group of \(G\) and denoted \(\Aut(G)\text{.}\)

    Remark 2.26.

    A homomorphism from a group to itself is called an endomorphism

    Example 2.27.

    In Example¬†2.19 we saw that the identity map is a group isomomorphism from a group to itself, making \(\id\) an automorphism. This may prove (pun intended) useful in the following proposition.

    Proposition 2.28. The Automorphism Group.

    The set of automorphisms of \(G\text{,}\) denoted \(\operatorname{Aut}(G)\text{,}\) is a group under composition.

    Exploration 2.29. \(\Aut(\Z)\).

    \(\Aut(\Z)\cong\Z/2\)

    Remark 2.30.

    Notice that we have actually shown that the composition of automorphisms is an automorphism, both through Corollary¬†2.23 (as automorphisms are isomorphisms) and Proposition¬†2.28 (as groups are closed under their operation)
    Automorphism groups may also give us information about whether or not our group is abelian.

    Exploration 2.31. Abelian Groups and Automorphisms.

    Let \(G\) be a group.
    1. The map \(\varphi:G\to G\) given by \(\varphi(a)=a^{-1}\) for all \(a\in G\) is an automorphism if and only if \(G\) is abelian.
    2. If \(\Aut(G)=\{e\}\) then \(G\) is abelian and every non-identity element of \(G\) has order \(2\text{.}\)

    Definition 2.32. Inner Automorphism.

    Let \(G\) be a group and \(x\in G\text{.}\) The function \(\psi_x:G\to G\) defined by \(\psi_x(a)=xax^{-1}\) is called the inner automorphism of \(G\) induced by \(x\text{.}\) The set of inner automorphisms of \(G\) is denoted \(\Inn(G)=\{\psi_x\mid x\in G\}\text{.}\)
    Before we get too ahead of ourslves, let's make sure this is indeed an automorphism to avoid any potential awkwardness down the line.

    Theorem 2.33. The Conjugation Automorphism.

    Let \(G\) be a group and \(x\in G\text{.}\) Then \(\psi_x\in \operatorname{Aut}(G)\) for all \(x\in G\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    Notice \(\psi_g(xy) = gxyg^{-1} = gxg^{-1} gyg^{-1} = \psi_g(x) \psi_g(y)\text{,}\) proving that \(c_g\) is a homomorhism from \(G\) to itself.
    Observe that for any \(g, h \in G\text{,}\) we have \(\psi_g \circ \psi_h = \psi_{gh}\) since \(\psi_g(c_h(x)) = ghxh^{-1} g^{-1} = ghx(gh)^{-1} = \psi_{gh}(x)\text{.}\) Also, \(\psi_e = \operatorname{id}_G\text{,}\) which is obvious. It follows that \(\psi_g \circ \psi_{g^{-1}} = \operatorname{id}_G\) and \(\psi_{g^{-1}} \circ \psi_g = \operatorname{id}_G\text{.}\) This proves \(\psi_g\) has a two-sided inveerse and hence is an isomorphism. In fact, it shows that \((\psi_g)^{-1} = \psi_{g^{-1}}\text{.}\)

    Remark 2.34.

    Note that as \(\psi_x:G\to G\) is bijective it is, by definition, a permutation of \(G\text{.}\) This might come up later...
    Finally, we end with two results that will prove invaluable later in the course. Though we won't see them for some time, proving them now will be good practice and save us time later on.

    Theorem 2.35. \(\Aut(C_n)\).

    The automorphism group of \(C_n\) is isomorphic to the multiplicative group of units of \(\Z/n\) via the map
    \begin{equation*} \varphi: (\mathbb{Z}/n^\times, \cdot) \to \operatorname{Aut}(C_n), \quad \varphi([j]_n)=[ y\mapsto y^j]. \end{equation*}
    where \(\mathbb{Z}/n^\times=\{[j]_n\mid \operatorname{gcd}(j,n)=1\}\text{.}\)
    In particular:

    Corollary 2.36. \(\Aut(C_p)\).

    If \(p\) is prime the automorphism group of \(C_p\) is cyclic, namely \(\operatorname{Aut}(C_p)\cong C_{p-1}\text{.}\)

    1. June 2020 (2).

    Let \(G\) be a group of order \(2m\) for some odd integer \(m > 1\text{.}\) Let \(\Perm(G)\) be the group of permutations on the elements of \(G\) (i.e., the group of bijective functions from G to G). You may use without proof that \(\Perm(G)\cong S_{2m}\text{.}\)
    1. Let \(f : G \to \Perm(G)\) be given by \(f (g) = \l_g\) for all \(g \in G\text{,}\) where \(\l_g : G \to G\) is defined by \(\l_g(a) = ga\) for all \(a \in G\text{.}\) Prove that \(f\) is an injective group homomorphism.
    2. Prove that if \(g \in G\) is an element of order \(2\) then \(\l_g\) is a product of \(m\) transpositions.
    3. Prove \(G\) contains a subgroup of index \(2\) and is consequently not simple.
    Hint.
    For part (3), consider the inverse image under \(f\) of the subgroup of \(\Perm(G)\) consisting of the even permutations.
