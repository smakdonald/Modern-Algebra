    Section 1.5 Group Homomorphisms

    Subsection Homomorphism? I Barely Know em!

    In the end, the character of a civilization is encased in its structures.
    Frank Gehry
    The Greek roots "homo" and "morph" together mean "same shape".

    Definition 1.58. Group Homomorphism.

    If \(G\) and \(H\) are groups, a homomorphism from \(G\) is \(H\) is a function \(\varphi: G \to H\) such that
    \begin{equation*} \varphi(x *_G y) = \varphi(x) *_H \varphi(y)\text{,} \end{equation*}
    where \(*_G\) and \(*_H\) denote the multiplication rules for \(G\) and \(H\text{,}\) respectively.

    LaTeX:\(\varphi\).

    In LaTeX, \(\varphi\) is written using\varphi.

    Convention 1.59. Homomorphism Notation: \(\varphi\) vs. \(f\).

    We use \(\varphi\) to denote an arbitrary homomorphism instead of \(f\) to differentiate between homormorphisms and ordinary functions.
    Some folk use \(f\) for homomorphisms as well as functions that are not homomorphisms. This is usually harmless, we avoid doing so here only to remove as much ambiguity as possible.
    Intuitively, a group homomorphism preserves the algebraic structure of the group, while allowing us to compare and relate different groups. In this way, group homomorphisms allow us to study the properties of groups by comparing them to other groups that we already understand well.

    Example 1.60. Homormophism Examples.

    1. The identity map is a group homomorphism for any group \(G\text{,}\) this is known as the trivial homomorphism.
    2. The zero map is a group homomorphism from \(\C\to\C\text{.}\)
    3. The sign homomorphism \(\sign: S_n\to \{-1,1\}\) that sends even permuations to \(1\) and odd permutations to \(-1\) is a homomorphism.
    Answer.
    1. Let \(x,y\in G\text{,}\) and let \(\id_G\) denote the identity map on \(G\text{.}\) Then
      \begin{align*} \id_G(xy) &= xy && \text{Definition of }\id_G \text{ on } xy\\ &= \id_G(x)\id_G(y) && \id_G(x)=x \text{ and } x \id_G(y)=y \end{align*}
      Thus \(\id_G\) is operation preserving, making it a group homomorphism for any group \(G\text{.}\)
    2. Let \(x,y\in \C\text{,}\) and let \(0_\C\) denote the zero map. As \(0_\C(xy) = 0 = 0_\C(x)0_\C(y)\text{,}\) \(0_\C\) is indeed a group homomorphism.
    3. First we note that \(\{-1,1\}\) is indeed a group, as \(\{-1,1\}=\Z^\times\text{,}\) as seen in Example1.15. Let \(\sigma,\tau\in S_n\text{.}\) We proceed via cases.
      First, suppose both \(\sigma\) and \(\tau\) are even permutations. Thus \(\sigma\tau\) is an even permutation by Proposition1.47, and hence
      \begin{equation*} \sign(\sigma)\sign(\tau) = (1)(1) = (1) = \sign(\sigma\tau). \end{equation*}
      Now suppose \(\sigma\) is even and \(\tau\) is odd. Thus \(\sigma\tau\) is an odd permutation by Proposition1.47, and hence
      \begin{equation*} \sign(\sigma)\sign(\tau) = (1)(-1) = (-1) = \sign(\sigma\tau). \end{equation*}
      Next, suppose \(\sigma\) is odd and \(\tau\) is even. Thus \(\sigma\tau\) is an odd permutation by Proposition1.47, and hence
      \begin{equation*} \sign(\sigma)\sign(\tau) = (-1)(1) = (-1) = \sign(\sigma\tau). \end{equation*}
      Finally, suppose both \(\sigma\) and \(\tau\) are odd permutations. Thus \(\sigma\tau\) is an even permutation by Proposition1.47, and hence
      \begin{equation*} \sign(\sigma)\sign(\tau) = (-1)(-1) = (1) = \sign(\sigma\tau). \end{equation*}
      This shows \(\sign\) is a homomorphism in all cases.

    Exercise 1.61.

    1. The exponential map \(\exp: ({\mathbb{R}}, +) \to (\mathbb{R}^\times, \cdot)\) is a homomorphism.
    2. The natural log map \(\ln: (\mathbb{R}^\times, \cdot) \to ({\mathbb{R}}, +)\) is a homomorphism.
    3. For any positive integer \(n\text{,}\) the map determinant map \(\det: \operatorname{GL}_n(\C) \to (\C^\times, \cdot)\) is a group homomorphism.
    Specifically, homomorphisms will preserve the additive structure, such as the identity element and inverse elements.

    Theorem 1.62. Homomorphism Preservations.

    If \(\varphi: G \to H\) is a homomorphism of groups, then the following hold:
    1. \(\displaystyle \varphi(e_G) = e_H\)
    2. \(\varphi(x^{-1}) = \varphi(x)^{-1}\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    Let \(\varphi: G \to H\) be a homomorphism of groups.
    1. Observe:
      \begin{align*} \varphi(e_G) &= \varphi(e_Ge_G)\\ &= \varphi(e_G)\varphi(e_G) && \knowl{./knowl/xref/def-ghom.html}{\text{Definition 1.58}}\\ \varphi(e_G)\varphi(e_G)\inv &= \varphi(e_G)\varphi(e_G)\varphi(e_G)\inv &&\text{multiply by }\varphi(e_G)\inv\\ e_H &= \varphi(e_G) \end{align*}
    2. Let \(x\in G\text{.}\) I claim that \(\varphi(x\inv)\) is the inverse of \(\varphi(x)\) in \(H\text{:}\)
      \begin{align*} \varphi(x)\varphi(x\inv) &= \varphi(xx\inv) && \knowl{./knowl/xref/def-ghom.html}{\text{Definition 1.58}}\\ &= \varphi(e_G)\\ &= e_H && \knowl{./knowl/xref/gp-ident-pres.html}{\text{Part 1}} \end{align*}
      Thus \(\varphi(x\inv)\) and \(\varphi(x)\inv\) are both inverses of \(\varphi(x)\) in \(H\text{,}\) and so \(\varphi(x^{-1}) = \varphi(x)\inv\text{,}\) since inverses are unique.

    Exercise 1.63.

    Let \(\varphi: G \to H\) be a homomorphism of groups, and let \(g\in G\text{.}\) Then \(\varphi(g^n)=\varphi(g)^n\text{.}\)

    Theorem 1.64. Compositions of Homomorphisms.

    If \(\varphi:G\to H\) and \(\phi:H\to K\) are group homomorphisms, the composition \(\phi\circ\varphi:G\to K\) is a group homomorphism.

    Proof.

    Suppose \(\varphi:G\to H\) and \(\phi:H\to K\) be group homomorphisms, and let \(x,y\in G\text{.}\)
    \begin{align*} \phi\circ\varphi(xy) &= \phi\circ\left(\varphi(x)\varphi(y)\right) && \knowl{./knowl/xref/def-ghom.html}{\text{Definition 1.58}} \text{ applied to } \varphi \\ &= \phi\left(\varphi(x)\varphi(y)\right) && \knowl{./knowl/xref/def-composition.html}{\text{Definition A.20}} \\ &= \phi\left(\varphi(x)\right)\phi\left(\varphi(y)\right) && \knowl{./knowl/xref/def-ghom.html}{\text{Definition 1.58}} \text{ applied to } \phi\\ &= \left(\phi\circ\varphi(x)\right)\left(\phi\circ\varphi(y)\right) && \knowl{./knowl/xref/def-composition.html}{\text{Definition A.20}} \end{align*}
    Thus \(\phi\circ\varphi:G\to K\) is a group homomorphism.

    Exercise 1.65. \(\Hom\) Sweet \(\Hom\).

    Definition 1.69. Group Ismorphism.

    A homomorphism \(\varphi: G \to H\) is called an isomorphism if there exists a homomorphism \(\phi: H \to G\) such that \(\varphi \circ \phi = \operatorname{id}_H\) and \(\phi \circ \varphi = \operatorname{id}_G\text{.}\)
    If \(\varphi:G\to H\) is an isomorphism, \(G\) and \(H\) are called isomorphic, written \(G\cong H\text{.}\)

    LaTeX:\(\cong\).

    In LaTeX, \(\cong\) is written using \cong.
    Intuitively, a group isomorphism establishes a one-to-one correspondence between the elements of two groups, such that they have exactly the same algebraic structure. This means that the groups are essentially the same, up to a relabeling of their elements.

    Theorem 1.70. Equivalent Isomorphism Characterization.

    Suppose \(\varphi: G \to H\) is a group homomorphism. Then \(\varphi\) an isomorphism if and only if \(\varphi\) is bijective (one-to-one and onto).

    Proof.

    \((\Rightarrow)\) Suppose \(\varphi\) an isomorphism. Then there exists a homomorphism \(\phi: H \to G\) such that \(\varphi \circ \phi = \operatorname{id}_H\) and \(\phi \circ \varphi = \operatorname{id}_G\text{.}\) Thus \(\varphi\) is invertible. By TheoremA.21 \(\varphi\) is bijective.
    (\(\Leftarrow\)) Suppose \(\varphi\) is bijective homomorphism. By TheoremA.21 \(\varphi\) is invertible, meaning there exists some function \(f: H \to G\)
    4
    Notice we use \(f\text{,}\) not \(\phi\text{!}\) We are not able to assume \(f\) is a homomorphism, we must prove this!
    such that \(\varphi \circ f = \operatorname{id}_H\) and \(f \circ \varphi = \operatorname{id}_G\text{.}\)
    I claim \(f\) is actually a homomorphism: For \(x,y \in H\) we have
    \begin{align*} \varphi(f(xy)) &= xy && \varphi\circ f = \operatorname{id}_H\\ &= \varphi(f(x))\varphi(f(y)) && \varphi\circ f = \operatorname{id}_H\\ &= \varphi(f(x)f(y)) && \varphi \text{ is a } \knowl{./knowl/xref/def-ghom.html}{\text{homomorphism}} \end{align*}
    Thus \(\varphi(f(xy))=\varphi(f(x)f(y))\) Since \(\varphi\) is bijective it is injective, and thus \(f(xy) = f(x)f(y)\text{,}\) making \(f\) a homomorphism.

    Remark 1.71.

    Moving forward, we will essentially treat Theorem1.70 as the de-facto definition of a group isomorphism.     Theorem 1.74. Isomorphism Invariants.

    Let \(G\) and \(H\) be groups. If \(\varphi:G\to H\) is an isomorphism, then the following hold:
    1. \(|G|=|H|\text{.}\)
    2. \(|g|=|\varphi(g)|\) for all \(g\in G\) and \(|h|=|\varphi\inv(h)|\) for all \(h\in H\text{.}\)
    3. \(G\) is abelian if and only if \(H\) is abelian.

    Proof.

    Let \(\varphi:G\to H\) be an isomorphism of groups with inverse \(\varphi\inv\text{.}\)
    1. By Theorem1.70 \(\varphi\) is a bijective function between \(G\) and \(H\text{.}\) Thus \(|G|=|H|\text{.}\)
    2. Let \(g\in G\) and \(h\in H\text{,}\) and let \(m,n\) denote the orders of \(g,h\text{,}\) respectively. Thus
      \begin{align*} \varphi(g)^m &= \varphi(g^m) && \knowl{./knowl/xref/exe-grp-hom-order-split.html}{\text{Exercise 1.63}}\\ &= \varphi(e_G) && m \text{ is the order of } g\\ &= e_H && \knowl{./knowl/xref/thm-grphomom-preservations.html}{\text{Theorem 1.62}} \end{align*}
      and
      \begin{align*} \varphi\inv(h)^n &= \varphi\inv(h^n) && \knowl{./knowl/xref/exe-grp-hom-order-split.html}{\text{Exercise 1.63}}\\ &= \varphi\inv(e_H) && n \text{ is the order of } h\\ &= e_G && \knowl{./knowl/xref/thm-grphomom-preservations.html}{\text{Theorem 1.62}} \end{align*}
      Thus \(\varphi(g)^m=e_H\) and \(\varphi\inv(h)^n=e_G\text{.}\)
      I claim \(m\) and \(n\) are the smallest positive integers where this holds. Suppose by way of contradiction there exist \(m'<m\) and \(n'<n\) such that \(\varphi(g)^{m'}=e_H\) and \(\varphi\inv(h)^{n'}=e_G\text{.}\) But then
      \begin{align*} g^{m'} &= \varphi\inv \varphi(g^{m'}) \\ &= \varphi\inv (e_H) && \varphi(g)^{m'}=e_H\\ &= e_G && \knowl{./knowl/xref/thm-grphomom-preservations.html}{\text{Theorem 1.62}} \end{align*}
      and
      \begin{align*} h^{n'} &= \varphi\varphi\inv(h^{n'}) \\ &= \varphi(e_G) && \varphi\inv(h)^{n'}=e_G\\ &= e_H && \knowl{./knowl/xref/thm-grphomom-preservations.html}{\text{Theorem 1.62}} \end{align*}
      As \(m'<m\) and \(n'<n\text{,}\) this contradicts \(|g|=m\) and \(|h|=n\text{.}\)
    3. \((\Rightarrow)\) Suppose \(G\) is abelian, and let \(x,y\in H\text{.}\)
      \begin{align*} xy &= \varphi\varphi\inv(xy)\\ &= \varphi\varphi\inv(x)\varphi\inv(y) && \knowl{./knowl/xref/def-ghom.html}{\text{Definition 1.58}} \\ &= \varphi\varphi\inv(y)\varphi\inv(x) && G \text{ abelian }, \varphi\inv(x), \varphi\inv(y)\in G, \\ &= \varphi\varphi\inv(yx) && \knowl{./knowl/xref/def-ghom.html}{\text{Definition 1.58}} \\ &= yx \end{align*}
      Thus every element two elements in \(H\) commute, making \(H\) abelian.
      \((\Leftarrow)\) Suppose \(H\) is abelian, and let \(x,y\in G\text{.}\)
      \begin{align*} xy &= \varphi\varphi\inv(xy)\\ &= \varphi\varphi\inv(x)\varphi\inv(y) && \knowl{./knowl/xref/def-ghom.html}{\text{Definition 1.58}} \\ &= \varphi\varphi\inv(y)\varphi\inv(x) && H \text{ abelian }, \varphi\inv(x), \varphi\inv(y)\in H, \\ &= \varphi\varphi\inv(yx) && \knowl{./knowl/xref/def-ghom.html}{\text{Definition 1.58}} \\ &= yx \end{align*}
      Thus every element two elements in \(G\) commute, making \(G\) abelian.

    Theorem 1.77. Isomorphisms form Equivalence Relation.

    1. \(\displaystyle G\cong G\)
    2. If \(G\cong H\text{,}\) then \(H\cong G\)
    3. If \(G\cong H\) and \(H\cong K\text{,}\) then \(G\cong K\)

    Proof.

    1. The identity map from \(G\to G\) is an isomorphism
    2. Suppose \(\varphi:G\to H\) is an isomorphism. Then there exists a homomorphism \(\phi: H \to G\) such that \(\varphi \circ \phi = \operatorname{id}_H\) and \(\phi \circ \varphi = \operatorname{id}_G\text{.}\) Thus \(\phi\) is an isomorphism from \(H\) to \(G\text{,}\) and so \(H\cong G\text{.}\)
    3. Let \(\varphi: G\to H\) and \(\phi: H\to K\) be isomorphisms. From Theorem1.64 we see \(\phi\circ\varphi\) is a homomorphism of groups. As both \(\varphi\) and \(\phi\) are injective, \(\phi\circ\varphi\) is injective by TheoremA.21. Similarly, as both \(\varphi\) and \(\phi\) are surjective, \(\phi\circ\varphi\) is surjective by TheoremA.21. Thus \(\phi\circ\varphi\) is a bijective homomorphism from \(G\) to \(K\text{,}\) we see \(G\cong K\) by Theorem1.70

    Remark 1.78.

    The quotation marks in the title of this result are important. Equivalence relations as we know them are only defined on sets, but there is no set of all groups, much as there is no set of all sets. This dips into the realm of Russels Paradox and higher category theory, which we will steer clear of for the time being.

    Proposition 1.82. The Automorphism Group.

    The set of automorphisms of \(G\text{,}\) denoted \(\operatorname{Aut}(G)\text{,}\) is a group under composition.

    Proof.

    Let \(\varphi, \phi, \psi\in\Aut(G)\text{.}\) As function composition is associative, we see \((\varphi\circ\phi)\circ\psi = \varphi\circ(\phi\circ\psi)\text{.}\)
    Recall from Example1.80 that \(\id:G\to G\) is an automorphism. I claim \(\id\) is the identity element of \(\Aut(G)\text{.}\) Let \(\varphi\in\Aut(G)\text{.}\) We will show \(\varphi\circ \id=\varphi=\id\circ \varphi\text{.}\) As all functions share the same domain and co-domain, we need only show that the functions map elements equally, Let \(x \in G\text{,}\) and observe:
    \begin{align*} \varphi\circ \id(x) &= \varphi(\id(x)) \\ &= \varphi(x) \\ &= \id(\varphi(x)) \\ &= \id\circ\varphi(x) \end{align*}
    Thus \(\id\) is indeed the identity element in \(\Aut(G)\text{.}\)
    Let \(\varphi\in\Aut(G)\text{.}\) As \(\varphi\) is an isomorphism, there exists a homomorphism \(\phi: G \to G\) such that \(\varphi \circ \phi = \operatorname{id}\) and \(\phi \circ \varphi = \operatorname{id}\text{.}\) Thus \(\phi\in\Aut(G)\text{,}\) making it the inverse of \(\varphi\text{.}\)

    proving that \(\psi_g\) is a homomorhism from \(G\) to itself.

    \begin{align*} \psi_g(\psi_h(x)) &= \psi_g(hxh^{-1}) && \text{apply }\knowl{./knowl/xref/def-inner-automorphisms.html}{\text{Definition 1.86}} \text{ for } \psi_h \\ &= ghxh^{-1} g^{-1} && \text{apply } \knowl{./knowl/xref/def-inner-automorphisms.html}{\text{Definition 1.86}} \text{ for } \psi_g \\ &= ghx(gh)^{-1} && \knowl{./knowl/xref/thm-group-inverses.html}{\text{Theorem 1.20}}\\ &= \psi_{gh}(x) && \text{apply }\knowl{./knowl/xref/def-inner-automorphisms.html}{\text{Definition 1.86}} \text{ for } \psi_{gh} \end{align*}

    As \(\psi_g \circ \psi_h = \psi_{gh}\text{,}\) it follows \(\psi_g \circ \psi_{g^{-1}} = \operatorname{id}_G\) and \(\psi_{g^{-1}} \circ \psi_g = \operatorname{id}_G\text{.}\) This proves \(\psi_g\) has a two-sided inverse and hence is an isomorphism.

    Thus \(\psi_g\in \operatorname{Aut}(G)\) for all \(g\in G\text{.}\)
